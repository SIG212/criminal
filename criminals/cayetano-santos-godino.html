Background

Cayetano Santos Godino was born in Buenos Aires in 1896 to Italian immigrants, the seventh of nine children. His home life was defined by poverty and violence; his father, Fiore, was an alcoholic who frequently beat Cayetano and his siblings. The boy suffered from various childhood ailments, including a severe case of rickets that left him with his distinctive, oversized ears. He was illiterate, prone to truancy, and by the age of seven, had already demonstrated a marked capacity for cruelty. His initial transgressions were directed at animals, a common precursor in such cases, but soon escalated. In 1904, he was arrested for the first time after leading a two-year-old child to a ditch and throwing stones at him. The incident was dismissed as childish misbehavior. He was briefly institutionalized but was released, returning to the streets of a rapidly expanding city that had little time or resources for a troubled boy from the slums.

The Crimes

His methods developed a grim consistency. Godino targeted children who were too young to resist or cry for help, typically toddlers he found playing unattended. He would lure them to secluded locations—vacant lots, abandoned buildings—with the promise of candy. The attacks were characterized by strangulation, usually with a length of cord or rope known locally as a *piolín*. Between 1906 and his final arrest in 1912, he was linked to at least four murders and seven attempts. In January 1912, he killed 13-year-old Arturo Leston, beating him and leaving him for dead with a rope around his neck; the boy died in hospital days later. Later that year, he murdered five-year-old Reyna Vainicoff and five-year-old Virginia Blanco. His final victim was Jesualdo Giordano, aged three. On December 3, 1912, Godino led the boy from his parents’ storefront to an empty house. There, he attempted to strangle him with a cord. When the child continued to struggle, Godino found a large nail and hammered it into the boy’s temple.

Evasion

For six years, Godino operated without significant impediment from the authorities. His youth was his primary shield; the concept of a child serial killer was alien to the public and law enforcement alike. The Buenos Aires police force of the era was ill-equipped to identify patterns in what appeared to be isolated, random acts of violence against children in disparate neighborhoods. Institutional responses were fragmented and ineffective. When Godino was occasionally detained for lesser acts, such as arson or assault, his small stature and age led officials to place him in correctional facilities for minors, from which he was invariably released. The city’s social fabric also offered a degree of cover. In the poor, dense immigrant communities where he operated, children frequently played without direct supervision, and the disappearance of one for a few hours would not immediately trigger a comprehensive search.

Capture

The murder of Jesualdo Giordano proved to be the final act. After killing the boy, Godino attended the child’s wake the following evening, where he approached the grieving father to ask for details about the discovery of the body. His morbid curiosity and presence at the scene, coupled with his prior record of violence, drew the attention of police investigators. He was arrested on December 4, 1912, and provided a full confession. Without emotion, he led officers to the crime scene and recounted the details of his other attacks. The trial became a national spectacle, forcing a confrontation with the uncomfortable reality of juvenile depravity. Prominent criminologists, including Dr. Victor Mercante, examined him, applying the theories of Cesare Lombroso to his physical deformities, particularly his ears. Deemed criminally responsible despite his age, he was sentenced to life imprisonment at the Ushuaia penal colony, a notoriously harsh prison at the southern tip of the continent.

Legacy

The case of Cayetano Santos Godino left a permanent mark on Argentine society. It exposed the profound inadequacies of the juvenile justice system and catalyzed a national debate on the nature of evil, criminal responsibility, and social neglect. The crimes of "Petiso Orejudo" forced a re-evaluation of the perceived innocence of childhood and became a foundational case study in Latin American criminology. Godino remained imprisoned for the rest of his life. He died in 1944 at the age of 48 from an internal hemorrhage. While the official report was inconclusive, prison lore holds that he was beaten to death by fellow inmates, an act of retribution for killing one of their cherished prison cats. It was a final, brutal symmetry for a life that began with cruelty to animals and ended at the hands of men.
