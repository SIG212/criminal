Background

Moses Sithole was born in Vosloorus, Boksburg, in 1964, into a South Africa defined by the rigid structures of apartheid. His early life was marked by instability; following his father's death and his mother's abandonment, he and his siblings were placed in an orphanage, where he later alleged he was subjected to abuse. As a young man, he found work in the gold mines, a common path for many black South Africans of his generation. His first significant conflict with the law resulted in a conviction for rape, for which he was sentenced to a term in prison. In 1993, he was released on parole. To outward appearances, he was a charismatic and well-spoken individual, a man who did not project an immediate sense of threat. He re-entered a society in the midst of a seismic political transformation, one characterised by widespread hope but also by profound economic uncertainty and institutional flux.

The Crimes

His campaign of violence began in July 1994 and continued unabated until his capture in October 1995. Sithole developed a consistent and effective method for acquiring his victims. He established a non-existent organisation, Youth Against Human Abuse, and presented himself as a successful businessman looking to hire staff. This pretext allowed him to target his preferred victim type: young, unemployed black women desperate for work. He would arrange to meet them for a supposed job interview, often instructing them to meet him near a train station before walking with them to a more secluded location. He operated across a wide geographical area, earning the moniker "The ABC Killer" for his activities in Atteridgeville, Boksburg, and Cleveland, Johannesburg. Once isolated in the veld, he would rape and then strangle his victims, typically using their own clothing. The bodies were left where they fell, often discovered weeks or months later in advanced states of decomposition. One of the first victims identified in this series was Maria Monare, found near the Heriotdale train station in July 1994. In total, 38 women were killed in this manner.

Evasion

Sithole’s ability to evade capture for over a year was a direct consequence of the environment in which he operated. The newly formed South African Police Service (SAPS) was undergoing a massive restructuring, integrating previously separate and often hostile police forces into a single national entity. This transition created significant administrative and operational challenges. Communication between different police districts was poor, and a phenomenon known as "linkage blindness" prevented investigators in one jurisdiction from connecting their cases to nearly identical murders occurring in another. The crimes were treated as isolated incidents rather than the work of a single, mobile offender. Furthermore, forensic capabilities were limited, and the vast, open spaces of the Highveld provided Sithole with countless locations to commit his crimes and dispose of the bodies with a low immediate risk of discovery. He exploited the system’s fractures and the land’s geography with cold proficiency.

Capture

The end of Sithole's killing spree was precipitated by his own escalating confidence and a need for recognition. He began contacting the media, speaking with a journalist at *The Star* newspaper and claiming responsibility for the murders. In a final, hubristic act, he telephoned the police, identified himself as the killer, and demanded to speak with State President Nelson Mandela. That call, made from a public phone in Johannesburg, was traced. When officers from a specialised task force closed in, Sithole fled, leading to a confrontation during which he was shot and wounded before being taken into custody. His trial was a landmark case for the new South Africa. He pleaded not guilty, often appearing nonchalant or defiant in the courtroom. The prosecution built its case on the testimony of the few women who had escaped him, circumstantial evidence, and his own incriminating communications.

Legacy

Found guilty of 38 counts of murder, 40 of rape, and six of robbery, Moses Sithole was sentenced to a total of 2,410 years in prison. The case served as a brutal audit of the nascent post-apartheid state, exposing critical failures in the penal system that had granted him early parole and the policing system that failed to identify his pattern of offending. It highlighted the acute vulnerability of a generation of women caught in the economic precarity of the transition, whose search for legitimate employment was turned into a fatal trap. The immense sentence delivered by Judge David Carstairs was not a practical measure but a symbolic one—an attempt by the legal system to account for a loss so profound it could not be balanced. It was a formal recognition that the crimes were not just an assault on individuals, but on the fragile promise of the new society itself.
