Background

Roger Andermatt presented as an unremarkable figure in the quiet, regulated world of central Swiss geriatric care. By day, he was a nurse, a vocation that placed him in a position of trust with the elderly and infirm of Lucerne. He was perceived by colleagues as competent, if somewhat reserved, navigating the daily routines of long-term care facilities with professional detachment. This public-facing identity, however, was only one part of his existence. By night, this persona dissolved into that of "R.O.-Gee," a local DJ known for spinning techno and house music in the clubs of Lucerne. This parallel life offered a stark contrast to his work: where the nursing home was defined by quietude and decline, the club scene was one of vitality and noise. To those who knew him in either capacity, he appeared to be just another individual balancing a respectable career with a personal passion. There was nothing in his background or daily conduct to suggest a capacity for systematic violence.

The Crimes

The killings began in 1995 and continued for six years across several nursing homes where Andermatt was employed. His procedure was consistent and methodical. He targeted patients who were severely ill, often suffering from dementia, Alzheimer's, or other terminal conditions that made their deaths seem plausible. After selecting a victim, he would administer a high dose of a tranquilizer, typically a benzodiazepine, to render them sedated or unconscious. Once the patient was incapacitated, he would complete the act through asphyxiation, using a plastic bag or a pillow. This two-step method ensured there was no struggle and left minimal signs of foul play. Investigators later determined he was responsible for a confirmed total of 22 deaths. He operated without a clear external motive such as financial gain or overt sadism; he would later claim his actions were motivated by a desire to end the suffering of his patients, a self-appointed arbiter of life and death on the ward.

Evasion

Andermatt’s ability to operate undetected for six years was less a testament to his own cunning than a reflection of systemic vulnerabilities within the Swiss long-term care system. In an environment where death is a routine event, the passing of an elderly, frail patient rarely triggered suspicion. Attending physicians, often overworked and seeing numerous patients, were inclined to attribute the deaths to natural causes, citing the victims' pre-existing conditions on death certificates without ordering autopsies. The institutional culture of care homes at the time lacked the rigorous oversight protocols that might have identified a statistical anomaly in the mortality rates on a particular nurse’s shifts. Furthermore, Andermatt cultivated an image of a compassionate caregiver, a persona that deflected suspicion from his colleagues. He was the quiet nurse who sat with the dying, a presence that was misinterpreted as dedication rather than predation.

Capture

The pattern was finally broken not by a single piece of forensic evidence, but by an accumulation of circumstantial suspicion. In mid-2001, staff at one Lucerne-area facility noted an unusual mortality rate correlated with Andermatt's shifts. More unsettling were comments he reportedly made, sometimes seeming to predict which patients would soon pass away. A formal complaint was lodged by a concerned colleague, triggering a police investigation. When brought in for questioning, Andermatt’s professional composure fractured. He confessed, detailing the 22 murders he had committed over the years, explaining his methods and his self-professed "mercy" motive. The subsequent trial in 2005 became a national media event. The prosecution argued that his actions were born not of compassion but of a need for power and control over the most helpless. The court rejected his mercy-killing defense, convicting him on multiple counts of murder and sentencing him to life imprisonment, the maximum penalty under Swiss law.

Legacy

The case of Roger Andermatt sent a profound shock through Swiss society, challenging the nation’s self-image of order and security. It laid bare the critical oversight gaps in institutions entrusted with the care of the most vulnerable, prompting calls for reform in how deaths are certified and monitored in nursing facilities. The trial also forced a difficult public conversation about euthanasia, distinguishing Andermatt’s criminal actions from the legally sanctioned practice of assisted suicide for which Switzerland is known. His case became a textbook example of the "angel of mercy" archetype in serial homicide, a caregiver who perverts their role into one of a predator. Ultimately, the file on the Death-Keeper of Lucerne stands as a stark record of how easily violence can be masked by routine, and how the quietest, most unassuming environments can conceal the most significant harm.
