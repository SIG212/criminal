Background

Before she was known as an abbess, Mariam Soulakiotis was Marina Soulakiotou, a figure whose early life remains largely undocumented. She emerged into public record not as an individual but as a follower within a burgeoning religious movement. In the 1920s, she joined the Old Calendarists, a splinter group that vehemently rejected the Greek Orthodox Church's adoption of the Gregorian calendar. She became a disciple of the movement’s charismatic leader, Archbishop Matthew Karpathakis of Vresthena, and settled at the Panaghia Pefkovounogiatrissa Monastery he established in the rural outskirts of Keratea, near Athens. Within this isolated and self-regulating community, Soulakiotis distinguished herself through her piety and administrative acumen. Upon Archbishop Matthew’s death in 1939, she ascended to the position of abbess, inheriting absolute authority over the monastery and its growing population of nuns and lay followers.

The Crimes

The period of Soulakiotis’s leadership, from 1939 until her arrest in 1950, was defined by a systematic pattern of exploitation and abuse. The monastery’s primary targets were wealthy women, often widows or those without close family, who were persuaded to join the community in search of spiritual salvation. A prerequisite for entry was the complete renunciation of all worldly possessions; prospective nuns were required to sign over deeds to property, hand over jewellery, and transfer their savings to the monastery. Once inside, they were subjected to a regimen of extreme austerity that far exceeded conventional monastic practice. Investigators and trial witnesses would later describe a system of enforced starvation, where food was withheld as a tool of discipline. Physical beatings were common, and medical care was denied, even for serious illnesses like tuberculosis, which became rampant within the convent’s walls. The estimated number of deaths attributed to this neglect and abuse reached 177, a figure derived from a review of death certificates from the area, though the precise count remains a matter of historical debate.

Evasion

For over a decade, the activities within the monastery walls evaded official scrutiny. This was largely due to the convent’s dual isolation—geographical and ideological. Situated in a remote rural area, it was physically removed from the daily life of Athens. More significantly, its status as a schismatic Old Calendarist sect placed it outside the oversight of the mainstream Greek Orthodox Church and, by extension, the state, which was reluctant to interfere in the affairs of a religious minority. Soulakiotis maintained absolute control over her followers, intercepting mail and forbidding contact with the outside world. Families who attempted to visit or inquire about their relatives were turned away, told that the women had willingly severed all worldly ties. The deaths that occurred were attributed to natural causes or the rigours of a devout life, a plausible claim in an era of high mortality from diseases like tuberculosis.

Capture

The monastery’s closed system finally fractured in 1950. A complaint filed by Eugenia Margheti, whose mother had died after donating a substantial fortune to the convent, proved to be the catalyst that authorities could not ignore. Coupled with a rising number of similar reports from disillusioned former nuns and concerned relatives, her persistence prompted a police raid on the Keratea compound in December of that year. Officers discovered dozens of emaciated and ill women living in prison-like conditions. Soulakiotis was arrested and, over the course of several trials, faced charges ranging from homicide and fraud to the abuse of religious office. Testimony from survivors painted a damning picture of her methods. In 1951, she was convicted and ultimately received multiple life sentences. She maintained her innocence until the end, portraying herself as a martyr persecuted for her faith. She died of a stroke in Averoff Prison in 1957.

Legacy

The case of Mariam Soulakiotis served as a stark examination of the dangers inherent in unregulated, totalist institutions. It exposed a critical failure of oversight on the part of both the state and the established church, which had allowed a self-contained world of extreme abuse to flourish under the protection of religious freedom. In the context of post-Civil War Greece, a society grappling with profound social and political instability, the monastery had offered an illusion of certainty and spiritual purity that proved fatally attractive to the vulnerable. The affair laid bare the mechanisms of coercive control, demonstrating how faith and the promise of salvation could be weaponised to strip individuals not only of their material wealth but also their autonomy and, ultimately, their lives. It remains a singular case study in the pathology of power and the calculated exploitation of belief.
