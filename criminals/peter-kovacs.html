Background

Péter Kovács lived a life of unremarkable routine in Martfű, a small industrial town dominated by the Tisza Shoe Factory. Born in 1934, he was a product of his time and place: a labourer who found steady work as a truck driver for the factory, the economic and social nucleus of the community. He married and had a child, presenting the exterior of a conventional family man. To his neighbours and colleagues, Kovács was a known quantity, a fixture in the local landscape whose daily movements were dictated by factory shifts and delivery schedules. There was nothing in his public demeanour or personal history to suggest a capacity for extreme violence. His life was, by all accounts, circumscribed by the predictable rhythms of work and home in a planned socialist economy, a context in which individual aberrations were believed to have been smoothed over by collective purpose.

The Crimes

The violence began on July 22, 1957. A young woman returning from her shift at the shoe factory was attacked, bludgeoned, and sexually assaulted. She survived, but her trauma and the darkness of the attack rendered her identification of the perpetrator unreliable. Nevertheless, local police arrested a man named Ákos Réti, who was tried and convicted for the crime. With Réti imprisoned, the case was considered closed. For seven years, an illusion of safety returned to Martfű. Then, in 1963, the attacks resumed. The methodology was consistent: Kovács targeted women walking alone at night, often near the factory. The assaults were brutal, escalating from battery to murder. He would strike his victims with a hammer or other heavy object before strangling them, often with their own clothing. The bodies of at least four women were disposed of in the network of canals and drainage ditches surrounding the town. The victims were ordinary workers, indistinguishable from hundreds of others, their only vulnerability being their presence on a dark road at the wrong time.

Evasion

Kovács’s ability to operate for a decade was a direct result of institutional failure, fortified by ideological rigidity. The primary obstacle to his early capture was the wrongful conviction of Ákos Réti. With a man already serving a life sentence for the initial 1957 attack, any subsequent, similar crimes were treated by authorities as isolated incidents. The police were unwilling to entertain the possibility that they had imprisoned an innocent man and that the real perpetrator remained at large. This reluctance was compounded by a political doctrine that considered serial murder a pathology of decadent capitalist societies, not a phenomenon that could exist within the Hungarian People's Republic. The official narrative held that such crimes were impossible in their system, and therefore the pattern connecting the murders was not officially acknowledged. Each new victim was a separate tragedy, a new case file, rather than a data point in a terrifying sequence.

Capture

The series of attacks ended not through systemic police work, but because Kovács made a critical error. On June 20, 1967, he attacked another woman, but she managed to fight him off and escape. Her survival provided investigators with a clear and credible witness. More importantly, the lead investigator on this new case, a young detective named Zoltán Pál, began to notice troubling similarities to the supposedly solved crimes of the past. Defying his superiors, he reopened the old files. Forensic evidence from one of the murder scenes, previously overlooked, was found to match Kovács. Faced with the survivor’s testimony and irrefutable physical evidence, the state’s narrative collapsed. Kovács was arrested on August 11, 1967. Under interrogation, he confessed to his crimes in detail. His trial was swift, and he was sentenced to death. Péter Kovács was executed by hanging in 1968.

Legacy

The case of the "Monster of Martfű" served as a clinical study in the collision of criminal pathology with state ideology. It exposed the profound danger of a justice system more committed to its own infallibility than to the truth. The investigation revealed two distinct tragedies: the lives taken by Péter Kovács and the life stolen from Ákos Réti, who served eleven years in prison for a crime he did not commit before being exonerated. For the authorities, the case was a source of deep institutional embarrassment, forcing them to confront a type of criminality they had publicly declared extinct within their borders. It demonstrated that no political system is immune to the darkest aspects of human behaviour, and that the refusal to acknowledge an uncomfortable reality does not prevent its consequences. It simply ensures they are paid for by the innocent.
