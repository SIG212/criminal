Background

Pedro Rodrigues Filho was born in 1954 on a farm in Santa Rita do Sapucaí, Minas Gerais, with a cranial injury. The damage was inflicted in utero when his father, during an argument, kicked his pregnant mother in the abdomen. This act of violence was a prelude to the environment that would shape him. He grew up in a context of rural poverty where disputes were often settled directly and physically. His own entry into homicide occurred at the age of fourteen. After his father, a school guard, was fired for allegedly stealing food from the school kitchen, Rodrigues shot and killed the local vice-mayor with his grandfather’s shotgun. Shortly thereafter, he murdered another guard, whom he believed to be the actual thief. He then fled to Mogi das Cruzes in Greater São Paulo, where he began executing drug traffickers and involving himself in burglaries, marking the start of a self-appointed crusade against those he deemed undesirable.

The Crimes

His criminal activity escalated in São Paulo, where he established a pattern of targeting other criminals. Rodrigues claimed a personal code: he killed only traffickers, rapists, and murderers. This self-styled vigilantism culminated in a spree of killings after his pregnant girlfriend, Maria Aparecida Olympia, was murdered by a rival gang leader. In response, Rodrigues tracked down several members of the gang, torturing and executing them to extract the name of the man responsible. His most infamous act, however, took place within the prison system. After his own father was incarcerated for murdering his mother with a machete, Rodrigues arranged to be transferred to the same facility. There, he confronted and stabbed his father twenty-two times. In a confession that has since become central to his notoriety, he stated that he then carved out a piece of his father’s heart, chewed it, and spat it out. This was one of over forty-seven murders he would commit while incarcerated.

Evasion

For his initial crimes, Rodrigues did not so much evade detection as operate within a milieu where his actions went largely uninvestigated. His victims were part of the criminal underworld, individuals whose disappearances rarely prompted significant police inquiry during a period of intense social upheaval and high crime rates in Brazil. He moved within the transient and violent ecosystems of favelas, where the state’s presence was limited and its resources were stretched thin. After his capture, his ability to continue killing was a direct result of institutional failure within Brazil’s notoriously overcrowded and violent prison system. He was not hiding; rather, he was operating with a degree of impunity in an environment where inmate-on-inmate violence was endemic and staff were unable or unwilling to maintain control. He claimed he could identify men who had committed rape simply by their demeanor, and would then execute them.

Capture

Rodrigues was first arrested on May 24, 1973, at the age of nineteen. He was initially sentenced to 128 years in prison. As he continued to commit murders behind bars, his sentence grew with each new conviction, eventually exceeding 400 years. This figure, however, was largely symbolic. Under the Brazilian Penal Code at the time, the maximum prison sentence that could be served was thirty years. Despite the staggering number of his confirmed victims—seventy-one, though he claimed over one hundred—the law dictated a finite period of incarceration. He was ultimately released in 2007 after serving thirty-four years. He was briefly imprisoned again on other charges but was finally freed in 2018 after a total of forty-two years behind bars.

Legacy

The case of Pedro Rodrigues Filho exposed fundamental deficiencies in Brazil’s justice and penal systems. It highlighted a state apparatus incapable of preventing a prolific killer from continuing his work even while in its custody. To a segment of the Brazilian public, he became a grim folk hero, an avenger doing the "dirty work" the government could not. His post-prison life presented a uniquely modern coda to his story. He became a minor celebrity, launching a YouTube channel where he recounted his crimes, offered warnings to young people about a life of crime, and commented on current events. This transformation from serial killer to content creator illustrated a disquieting contemporary phenomenon: the monetization of true evil. On March 5, 2023, Rodrigues was shot and killed by two masked men outside his home in Mogi das Cruzes, bringing his life to a close with the same type of street violence he had so often administered himself.
