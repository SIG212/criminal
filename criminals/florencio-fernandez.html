Background

Florencio Fernández was, by most accounts, an unremarkable figure in the provincial landscape of 1950s Tucumán. Born into poverty and with limited education, he existed on the margins of society, working as a *changador*, or day laborer, when he could find employment. He was known to be solitary and quiet, a man who moved through the city without drawing attention. There was nothing in his known history—no prior record of significant violence, no outward signs of psychosis—that would have signaled the events to come. His early life was characterized by the kind of anonymity common to many living in precarious economic conditions in mid-century regional Argentina. He was a component of the city's background, a presence so ordinary as to be functionally invisible to the authorities and his neighbors.

The Crimes

The series of killings attributed to him, which terrorized the province for nearly a decade, followed a distinct and disturbing pattern. The perpetrator targeted women who were alone in their homes, forcing entry during the night. The initial attacks were typically violent and swift, involving strangulation or bludgeoning. What set the crimes apart, and what would ultimately define their place in public memory, was the act that followed. After the victim was deceased, the attacker would bite into her neck or throat. Forensic reports of the time noted that exsanguination was not the cause of death; the biting was a subsequent ritual. Crime scenes were often chaotic, but the consistent element was this specific, post-mortem mutilation. The victims were not from a single social class or neighborhood, making the attacks appear random and unpredictable to a population accustomed to more comprehensible forms of crime.

Evasion

Fernández evaded capture for years due to a confluence of factors, including his own unobtrusive nature and the limitations of law enforcement at the time. In the 1950s, forensic science in provincial Argentina was rudimentary. Fingerprinting was not systematically employed, and the concept of criminal profiling was nonexistent. Investigations were localized, with poor communication between different police precincts, making it difficult to connect disparate homicides into a single series. Fernández’s social invisibility was his greatest asset. As a laborer, his presence in various neighborhoods would not have seemed out of place. He could observe routines and identify vulnerable households without arousing suspicion. The lack of a clear motive—robbery was rarely the primary goal—further confounded investigators, who were searching for a rational explanation for irrational acts.

Capture

His eventual capture in 1960 was not the result of a sophisticated investigation but reportedly came after he was found disoriented and was subsequently linked to the crimes. Under interrogation, Fernández confessed to at least fifteen murders. In his confession, he allegedly provided details only the killer could know and explained his compulsion to drink his victims' blood. He was never subjected to a full public trial. Instead, a panel of psychiatrists diagnosed him with schizophrenia and declared him legally insane, incapable of understanding the nature of his actions. He was committed to a psychiatric institution, where he remained for the rest of his life. He died within the facility's walls in 1968, having never been formally convicted or sentenced in a criminal court.

Legacy

The case of Florencio Fernández ultimately reveals more about the societal and institutional responses to extreme violence than it does about the man himself. In the absence of a trial and a clear judicial narrative, his story was shaped by the press and public fear, which coalesced around the moniker "The Vampire of Tucumán." This label served to both sensationalize the crimes and place them outside the realm of normal human behavior, transforming a mentally ill man into a folkloric monster. The ambiguity of the official record, with some historians questioning the number of victims and the veracity of the confession, has allowed the legend to overshadow the documented facts. His file sits uncomfortably between criminal record and provincial myth, a testament to an era when the justice system and the psychiatric field lacked the tools to adequately process a case of such profound abnormality.
