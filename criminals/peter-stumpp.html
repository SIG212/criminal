Background

Peter Stumpp was a man of his time and place: a seemingly unremarkable farmer living in the rural vicinity of Bedburg, near Cologne, during the latter half of the 16th century. Born around 1525, he was a widower and a father, known within his small community as a common agriculturalist. Historical records portray him as a man integrated into the local social fabric, neither exceptionally wealthy nor destitute, who lived a life dictated by the seasons and the demands of the land. For decades, he was simply a neighbor. This veneer of provincial normalcy was essential to the longevity of his activities. In an era defined by religious conflict and social anxiety, Stumpp’s apparent conformity provided an effective shield, allowing him to operate without suspicion from those who shared his landscape and his daily routines. He was, by all outward appearances, one of them.

The Crimes

The violence attributed to him, spanning approximately twenty-five years from 1564 to 1589, was directed at the most vulnerable. His confessed victims included fourteen children, some of whom were his own relatives, and two pregnant women. His method was opportunistic, targeting individuals in the isolated fields and forests that surrounded Bedburg. The attacks were characterized by extreme physical trauma; victims were often dismembered, their bodies left in a state that suggested an animal attack. According to the confession extracted from him, the assaults escalated to acts of cannibalism. He claimed to have consumed the raw flesh of his victims, and in the case of the two expectant mothers, to have torn the fetuses from their wombs and eaten their hearts. The most disturbing detail of his confession concerned his own son, whose brain he claimed to have devoured. Alongside these human victims, Stumpp admitted to the slaughter of local livestock—sheep, lambs, and goats—acts which, while less grievous, would have significantly impacted the local agrarian economy and deepened the community's fear.

Evasion

For a quarter of a century, the disappearances and livestock killings were not connected to a human perpetrator. Stumpp’s ability to evade detection was a product of both his environment and the prevailing belief system of the age. The heavily wooded terrain of the Rhineland offered a natural cover for his movements and an easy place to conceal his actions. Furthermore, the absence of any formal, centralized law enforcement meant that investigations were localized and lacked sophisticated methodology. Disappearances in such a landscape were not entirely uncommon and could be attributed to accidents or natural causes. Crucially, the community had already supplied its own explanation for the savaged livestock and missing people: a wolf. The narrative of a preternaturally cunning and vicious wolf, or even a werewolf, took hold, directing all suspicion away from the human population. This collective belief in a supernatural predator provided Stumpp with the perfect camouflage, allowing him to operate not as a criminal hiding from the law, but as a man shielded by his community’s own folklore.

Capture

His eventual capture in 1589 was, according to contemporary accounts, a dramatic event. A group of local men, having set out to hunt the creature they believed was responsible for the killings, purportedly cornered a large wolf with their dogs. As they closed in, the animal was said to have transformed back into the human form of Peter Stumpp. Once in custody, he was subjected to interrogation under torture, a standard judicial practice of the period designed to extract a confession. Placed upon the rack, Stumpp admitted to the sixteen murders and the associated acts of cannibalism and incest. He further confessed to having practiced black magic since the age of twelve, claiming that the Devil had given him a magical girdle which allowed him to metamorphose into the likeness of a wolf. His trial was swift. On October 28, 1589, he was sentenced to be executed by breaking on the wheel. His flesh was torn with red-hot pincers ten times before his limbs were broken and he was finally beheaded. His daughter, Beell, and his mistress, Katherine Trop, were condemned as accomplices and were burned at the stake on the same day.

Legacy

The case of Peter Stumpp serves as a document of more than just a series of alleged crimes; it is an artifact of the early modern European mindset. The judicial process, reliant on a confession obtained through extreme duress, leaves the precise details of his guilt uncertain. It is impossible to separate the facts of his actions from the lurid, supernatural narrative that both he and his interrogators constructed. The case demonstrates the period’s fusion of criminal justice with theology, where a serial killer could be prosecuted not simply for murder, but for consorting with the Devil. The subsequent publication of a pamphlet in London detailing his crimes and execution ensured his notoriety across Europe, cementing the association between lycanthropy, satanism, and a magical transforming object in popular folklore. Ultimately, the affair reveals a society grappling with inexplicable evil, finding it more plausible to believe in a man who could become a monster than to confront the possibility that the monster had been a man all along.
