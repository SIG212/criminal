Background

Before his name was recorded in the criminal annals of Afghanistan, Abul Djabar was an unremarkable figure. He moved through the landscape of the 1960s, a period of relative calm under the monarchy of Mohammed Zahir Shah, without attracting official notice. In a nation where a significant portion of the population was transient—merchants, laborers, and pilgrims traveling between cities like Kabul, Kandahar, and Herat—Djabar’s presence was indistinct. He was one of many men traversing the expanding network of roads that were beginning to connect the country’s disparate regions. This very anonymity, coupled with a landscape that offered vast, unmonitored spaces, provided the conditions for his activities. He was not a man of property or established status; he existed on the periphery, a social position that allowed him to observe and interact with the vulnerable without generating suspicion. To those he encountered, he would have appeared as just another traveler seeking companionship or offering assistance on a long and often solitary journey.

The Crimes

His method was consistent and relied upon the exploitation of customary hospitality. Djabar targeted men and boys, often those traveling alone. He would approach them at bus stops, teahouses, or along the road, offering them a ride, a meal, or a place to rest. Once he had gained their trust and led them to a secluded location, the pretext of kindness was abandoned. The attacks involved sexual assault, followed by strangulation. His weapon of choice was the turban, a common article of clothing that he turned into a ligature. The bodies of his victims were typically left in shallow graves or discarded in ditches along the highways, where they were often discovered by accident weeks or months later. Investigators, when they were involved at all, were confronted with a series of seemingly disconnected homicides. The victims were often unidentified, their disappearances unnoticed for extended periods, making it difficult to establish a pattern. The official count of his victims, based on his later confession, stands at 65, but authorities at the time suspected the true number could have exceeded 300.

Evasion

Djabar’s ability to evade detection for so long was a direct consequence of the era’s limitations. In the 1960s, Afghanistan lacked a centralized, national police force with modern forensic capabilities. Law enforcement was a provincial affair, with little to no communication or data sharing between jurisdictions. A crime committed near Kandahar would not be linked to a similar one outside Kabul. Furthermore, there was no national system for tracking missing persons. The victims Djabar selected were often from poor, rural families, individuals whose absence might not trigger an immediate or effective alarm. He capitalized on the geography of his environment—a vast, rugged country where a body could lie undiscovered indefinitely. His operational pattern, moving between different regions, further frustrated any localized investigation. He was not a static predator but a mobile one, and in the context of mid-20th century Afghanistan, this mobility made him functionally invisible to the state.

Capture

His capture was not the result of a single forensic breakthrough but rather the culmination of growing police awareness that a serial predator was active. As reports of similarly executed murders began to coalesce, police in several jurisdictions started to suspect they were hunting one man. The precise details of his arrest are subject to some variation in reporting, but the most consistent accounts describe a police operation. An officer was deployed as an undercover decoy, posing as a young, affluent traveler to attract the killer’s attention. Djabar reportedly took the bait, approaching the officer with an offer of companionship. He was apprehended, and under interrogation, his composure broke. He provided a detailed confession, admitting to dozens of murders across the country and leading investigators to the locations of some of his victims. His trial was swift. Presented with his own extensive confession, the court found him guilty. In October 1970, Abul Djabar was executed by hanging at Pul-e-Charkhi prison in Kabul.

Legacy

The case of Abul Djabar exposed the critical vulnerabilities of a state undergoing modernization without the commensurate development of its civic and security infrastructure. The new roads that symbolized progress also created corridors for predation, and the traditional social codes of trust and hospitality were turned into weapons. His crimes underscored the profound disconnect between the central government in Kabul and the vast rural expanses of the country, where state presence was minimal and justice was an abstract concept. For a brief period, his name became a cautionary tale for travelers, a stark reminder that extreme human pathology could flourish in the open spaces between cities. Yet, due to the subsequent decades of war and upheaval in Afghanistan, his story has largely faded from public memory, becoming a footnote in criminal history—a case study of how a society’s structural weaknesses can permit extraordinary violence to go unchecked, and how quickly such histories can be buried by the greater turmoil that follows.
