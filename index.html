Background

Leonarda Cianciulli presented the face of unremarkable provincial life in Correggio, a small town in Italy’s Emilia-Romagna region. Born in 1894, her early years were marked by hardship and a sense of profound misfortune, including two reported suicide attempts. After her marriage, she and her husband Raffaele Pansardi eventually settled in Correggio, where she opened a small shop and gained a reputation as a respectable, if somewhat superstitious, matron. Her life was defined by a series of seventeen pregnancies that resulted in thirteen miscarriages or infant deaths. This history of loss fostered an extreme, almost pathological devotion to her four surviving children, particularly her eldest and favourite son, Giuseppe. Cianciulli was also a believer in fortune-telling and omens, and a palm reader had once told her that all of her children would die. This prophecy, combined with her past trauma, created the psychological bedrock for the events that were to follow.

The Crimes

The catalyst for the murders was the impending departure of Giuseppe for the Italian army in 1939, at the outset of the Second World War. Convinced that only human sacrifice could ensure his safety, Cianciulli identified her victims from among the lonely, middle-aged women who sought her counsel. The first was Faustina Setti, a spinster whom Cianciulli promised to find a husband for in another town. On September 17, 1939, Setti visited Cianciulli’s home for a final farewell, where she was given a glass of drugged wine and killed with an axe. Cianciulli then dismembered the body, rendering the fatty tissues into soap and, according to her later confession, mixing the dried blood with flour, sugar, and chocolate to bake into teacakes which she served to guests. A similar pattern followed with Francesca Soavi, who was promised a teaching job abroad, and finally with Virginia Cacioppo, a former soprano, who believed she was leaving for a secretarial position in Florence. Each woman was systematically killed, her assets liquidated by Cianciulli, and her disappearance explained away through letters the killer had them write before their deaths.

Evasion

Cianciulli’s methods were designed not just for murder, but for erasure. By having her victims write postcards to family and friends from their supposed new locations, she created a plausible narrative for their absence. She also carefully managed their financial affairs post-mortem, collecting money on their behalf and selling their furniture and possessions, which further cemented the illusion that they had simply moved away. The victims were chosen for their relative isolation; they were women on the periphery of the town's social fabric, whose departure would not raise immediate alarm. In the context of pre-war Italy, with its less sophisticated forensic and communication systems, and a national focus directed towards impending conflict, the disappearances went uninvestigated for over a year. Cianciulli continued her daily life as a shopkeeper and mother, her activities shielded by the very domesticity that made her crimes so incongruous.

Capture

The scheme unraveled with the final victim, Virginia Cacioppo. Unlike the others, Cacioppo had a concerned and persistent sister-in-law who did not believe the story of her sudden departure for Florence. She had last seen Cacioppo entering Cianciulli’s house and reported her suspicions to the authorities in Reggio Emilia. The subsequent police investigation, led by Superintendent of Police Luigi Serrao, began to scrutinize Cianciulli’s activities. Initially denying everything, Cianciulli confessed almost immediately when investigators presented evidence that linked her to the sale of Cacioppo’s belongings. Her confession was reportedly unflinching and meticulously detailed, with Cianciulli even correcting investigators on certain points of her procedure. The trial became a national sensation, where she remained impassive while describing the process of converting human remains into household products. She was found guilty and sentenced to thirty years in a criminal asylum and three years in prison.

Legacy

The case of Leonarda Cianciulli endures in Italian criminal annals not for the number of its victims, but for its unsettling fusion of the mundane and the macabre. It revealed a particular kind of vulnerability among women of that era, especially those without the protection of a close family network. More significantly, it presented a chilling study in the perversion of maternal instinct, where the archetypal role of the protective mother was warped into a rationale for calculated slaughter. The murders were not acts of passion but were executed with the cold practicality of a household chore. Cianciulli’s trial and testimony forced a confrontation with the idea that profound violence could germinate behind the most ordinary of facades, rooted not in obvious malice, but in a distorted form of love and a deep-seated belief in superstition.
