Day 010 — Hungary

Péter

Background

Péter Kovács was a transport worker at the Tisza Shoe Factory in Martfű, a small industrial town built on the principles of socialist productivity. By the mid-1950s, he was a settled man, married with a child, and regarded by his colleagues and neighbours as unremarkable. He fulfilled his duties at the factory, participated in the expected rhythms of community life, and presented the exterior of a dutiful citizen in the Hungarian People's Republic. There was nothing in his daily conduct or personal history to suggest a capacity for extreme violence. His life was enmeshed in the fabric of Martfű, a town where the factory whistle dictated the tempo of existence and individual anonymity was subsumed by the collective. This very ordinariness, this seamless integration into the social machinery of the era, would become his most effective camouflage. He was not an outsider or a visible deviant; he was a component of the system he would terrorize.

The Crimes

The sequence of violence began on July 22, 1957. A young woman, Erzsébet P. Túri, was attacked near the factory after her shift. She was bludgeoned with a heavy iron object, strangled, and sexually assaulted. The brutality was notable, but in the absence of a pattern, it was treated as an isolated, albeit savage, crime of passion. The investigation quickly focused on the victim’s former boyfriend, Ákos Réti, who confessed under intense interrogation. With Réti’s conviction and life sentence, the case was officially closed. For seven years, Kovács remained dormant. Then, in 1964, the attacks resumed. The modus operandi was consistent: lone women, typically factory workers, were ambushed at night on the secluded paths surrounding the factory complex. The assaults involved strangulation and sexual violence, often post-mortem. The victims were chosen seemingly at random, their only commonality being their vulnerability and their proximity to Kovács’s sphere of operation. The attacks grew in frequency, creating a palpable but officially unacknowledged fear within the community.

Evasion

Kovács’s ability to evade detection for a decade was a direct result of institutional and ideological failure. The primary obstacle to his capture was the prior conviction of Ákos Réti. For the state authorities, the 1957 murder was solved. To acknowledge a series of new crimes bearing the same signature would be to admit a catastrophic failure of the justice system—an admission the rigid, image-conscious Communist state was unwilling to make. Furthermore, the concept of a serial killer, a predator who killed compulsively and without a rational motive, was considered a symptom of decadent Western capitalism. Such pathology, official doctrine held, could not exist in a healthy socialist society. Local police were ill-equipped, both psychologically and methodologically, to hunt for a phantom who defied their understanding of criminality. Each new murder was treated as a discrete event, its connection to the others deliberately ignored to protect the integrity of the original, flawed investigation.

Capture

By 1967, the escalating frequency of the attacks rendered the official narrative untenable. The murder of a local council member’s daughter finally forced a serious, coordinated investigation. A provincial prosecutor and a team of detectives began to re-examine the string of unsolved homicides, eventually linking them to the 1957 case for which Réti was imprisoned. The investigation was systematic, focusing on the male workers at the Tisza Shoe Factory. During questioning, Péter Kovács, the unassuming transport worker, aroused suspicion. A search of his home uncovered incriminating evidence. Under interrogation, his composure fractured, and he provided a full confession to all the attacks, detailing events with a clinical detachment that stunned investigators. Following a trial that exposed the state’s profound errors, Kovács was found guilty. He was executed by hanging in 1968. Ákos Réti, having served eleven years for a crime he did not commit, was exonerated and released.

Legacy

The Kovács case served as a stark indictment of a political system that prioritized ideological purity over empirical reality. It revealed how the state’s refusal to acknowledge a problem—in this instance, the existence of a serial predator—directly enabled further violence, costing several women their lives and an innocent man his freedom. The murders in Martfű were not merely the work of one disturbed individual; they were a phenomenon allowed to fester within the blind spots of a totalitarian regime. The affair exposed the fallibility of a justice system predicated on forced confessions and the preservation of official infallibility. Ultimately, the case demonstrated a disquieting truth that transcends political systems: the capacity for profound violence can reside within the most ordinary of facades, and the greatest danger often lies not in what is seen, but in what a society refuses to look for.
